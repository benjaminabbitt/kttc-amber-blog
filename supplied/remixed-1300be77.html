Donelson Loop Trail — 3.4 Miles Activity

The signature experience at this park. The orange-blazed loop follows hand-dug Confederate earthwork trenches along the bluffs, passes the Confederate river batteries where massive cannons overlook Lake Barkley (the view is stunning), continues through replica log huts at the original fort site, past the monument to fallen soldiers, and returns through hardwood forest over small creek bridges. Resident bald eagles have nested in the fort area — worth watching for. AllTrails 4.6 ★ from 467 reviews.

~11:45 AM
Dover Hotel (Surrender House) + National Cemetery Historic Site

A 1.5-mile drive to 301 Petty St. This is where Confederate General Simon Buckner surrendered 14,000 troops to U.S. Grant on February 16, 1862 — the moment that made Grant a national hero and earned him the nickname "Unconditional Surrender" Grant. The exhibit room is open daily 10 AM–4 PM. The 1850s building is remarkably intact. The most historically charged 15 minutes at the park.

Immediately after, walk through Fort Donelson National Cemetery — over 650 Union soldiers buried here, with a historic carriage house. About 10 minutes.

Adventure Science Center Honor's Pick

Nashville's premier hands-on science museum, 3 miles south of the hotel (~10-min drive). Four floors of interactive exhibits: walk on the moon simulator (harness lifts you to simulate lunar gravity), climb through the ventricles of a giant beating heart, augmented reality mirror showing your own muscles and bones, the 4-story Adventure Tower with a bicycle-powered elevator and a walk-on piano, and the 63-foot full-dome Sudekum Planetarium for full-dome shows. 175+ exhibits — budget 90 minutes minimum.

Tip: Head to the upper floors first to beat crowds. The moon walk and Adventure Tower are the biggest hits for Honor's age group.

~11:00 AM
Stones River Battlefield Loop + Hazen Brigade Monument Activity

The 3.1-mile loop trail is flat and easy — mowed grass paths around farm fields with replica cannons and soldier silhouettes along the route. The highlight is Stop 5: Round Forest / Hell's Half Acre, where Colonel Hazen held the only section of the Union line that didn't break on Day 1 against four consecutive Confederate waves.

The Hazen Brigade Monument is the oldest surviving Civil War monument still standing in its original battlefield location — erected just six months after the battle in 1863, it also serves as the burial site for 45+ men of Hazen's brigade. Stand here and do the math: 32.7% casualties across both armies, more than Shiloh or Antietam.

Also visit Stones River National Cemetery — 6,850 interments, 2,562 unidentified. The tradition is to leave pennies on headstones for fallen service members. AllTrails 4.6 ★ from 1,416 reviews.

🗿 World's Largest Cedar Bucket — Cannonsburgh Village World's Largest

312 S Front St — a 5-minute drive from Joanie's, perfectly timed as Cannonsburgh opens at 1 PM on Sunday. Free admission. The bucket is 6 feet tall, 9 feet wide at the top, capable of holding 1,556 gallons. Built in 1887 as a promotional tool, it won blue ribbons at the 1893 Chicago World's Fair and the 1904 St. Louis World's Fair, where a beer company filled it with 1,566 gallons of beer and built a catwalk around the top for sampling. The pioneer village itself is worth 30 minutes: working gristmill, one-room schoolhouse, blacksmith shop, caboose, and wedding chapel — a genuine slice of rural Tennessee from the 1830s–1930s.
